A Simple Guide to How Credit Cards Really Work

If you’re new to credit cards or want a clearer understanding before choosing one, this page gives you the fundamentals — how cards operate, the features that matter, and what to look for as you get started.

Visit Choose.Creditcard

What Is a Credit Card?

A credit card lets you borrow money up to a set limit for everyday purchases. You can pay the balance in full each month or carry part of it forward and pay interest. Different cards offer different perks, fees and rules.

This page provides a clean, beginner-friendly introduction so you understand core concepts before comparing specific card offers.

The Key Concepts You Need to Know

All credit cards follow the same basic structure, but how they behave depends on your usage and the issuer’s policies. These are the building blocks that make everything else make sense.

  • Credit limit: The maximum amount you can borrow at once.
  • Statement balance: The amount you owe at the end of each billing cycle.
  • Minimum payment: The smallest amount required to keep the account in good standing.
  • APR: The interest rate applied if you carry a balance.
  • Fees: May include annual fees, FX fees, cash-advance fees or late-payment fees.

Rewards and Benefits — Optional, Not Guaranteed

Some cards offer cashback, points or miles. Others focus on low fees or credit-building. None of these features matter if the basics aren’t used well — paying on time and keeping balances low matter far more to your financial health.

Choosing the right type depends on your habits. Frequent travellers may benefit from travel cards, while casual spenders often prefer simple cashback options.

Smart Habits When Starting Out

When you first begin using credit, a few simple habits prevent costly mistakes. Paying your statement balance in full, keeping utilisation under 30% and avoiding unnecessary applications help strengthen your financial profile early.

Responsible use now sets the foundation for higher limits, better cards and lower borrowing costs later.

How to Compare Cards When You’re New

Feature What to Look For Why It Matters
Fees Annual fee, FX fee, penalty fees Low-fee cards are usually best when you're starting out.
APR Purchase APR and cash-advance APR If you ever carry a balance, APR determines your cost.
Credit Requirements Approval difficulty and score requirements Beginner cards usually have simpler requirements.
Rewards Simple cashback or basic earn structures Easier to use and understand when you’re new.
Protection Features Purchase protection and dispute rights Helpful if you're still developing good financial habits.
